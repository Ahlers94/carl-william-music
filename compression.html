Theory

COMPRESSION

A complete guide — from the controls to advanced techniques

A COMPRESSOR is a time-based volume automation tool, and the art is making the automation feel like it was performed, not programmed.

When you have a singer that has dynamic range, they might whisper in the verse and belt it in the choruses. So you'd have to manually move the fader down in the chorus. A compressor does that automatically.
The Controls
THRESHOLD

Like a fader set to the volume you want the compressor to kick on at. Set it to the level where the loud peaks begin, so the compressor only works when the signal exceeds that point.

ATTACK

The speed in milliseconds at which the compression will kick on once the signal hits the threshold.

Fast Attack < 5ms

The compressor catches the very first "crack" of a snare drum or the first consonant of a word. It sounds tighter and more controlled.

Slow Attack > 20–30ms

The compressor "misses" the initial punch of the sound. Great for drums or acoustic guitar — it lets the transient ring out before the volume gets pulled down. Makes things sound punchy.

RELEASE

The speed in ms at which the compressor returns to zero gain reduction, fully letting go of the signal.

Fast Release

The gain returns to zero gain reduction quickly. This makes the music feel more lively, but if it's too fast it creates a "pumping" effect.

Slow Release

The gain returns to zero gain reduction slowly. Makes the music feel glued together and smoother, as if the volume is rising and falling with the musical phrasing rather than reacting to every single note.

RATIO

The "strength" of the movement. At 4:1, for every 4dB of volume increase past the threshold, it is only increasing by 1dB.

1.5:1 – 2:1 Glue, warmth, barely noticeable. Great for bus compression and mixing.
4:1 – 6:1 Classic vocal and instrument control.
8:1 – 10:1 Getting into limiting territory. Very aggressive.
∞:1 That is a limiter. Nothing gets past the threshold, period.
KNEE
Hard Knee

Instantly applies the full ratio the moment the signal crosses the threshold.

Soft Knee

Applies some compression before the signal hits the threshold, gradually increasing the ratio until it hits the full amount. Good for acoustic guitars or vocals to make the compression feel invisible.

PEAK LIMITER

A final safety net. Even if your compressor is doing its job, the limiter is at the end of the signal chain to prevent digital clipping or blowing your speakers if anything tries to exceed the value you set.

GATE

Acts like a "silence" switch. If the signal falls below a certain volume, it cuts it off entirely — e.g., stopping the hum of a guitar amp between notes. Usually placed before the compressor in the signal chain.

EXPANDER

Like a gentle gate. Instead of cutting the sound to zero, it just turns it down further when it gets quiet. Great for cleaning up background noise in a vocal track without the choppy sound a hard gate can sometimes create.

GAIN REDUCTION METER

Watch this meter while you listen. The timing of the movement is as important as the depth.

▼ If it's barely moving then threshold is too high, or you're barely sending any signal.
▲ If it's pinned in the red or at the bottom either the threshold is too low or ratio is too aggressive. You're limiting your signal.
◆ Moving up and down with the music, pumping in time is where you want to be.
▸ Pumping to the quarter note or the bar? That's your release interacting with the tempo.
▸ Ducking hard on consonants and releasing on vowels? You may need to slow your attack.
▸ Barely moving on the verse but pinned in the chorus? That's normal on a dynamic vocal consider automating the threshold between sections rather than compromising both.
Advanced Techniques
MAKEUP / OUTPUT GAIN

When compression pulls down peaks, your overall perceived loudness drops. The Output or Makeup Gain knob is how you push it back up to unity level or beyond.

A huge rookie mistake is bypassing the compressor and thinking it sounds better. It almost always does, because the uncompressed signal is louder, and louder sounds better to our ears. Always match levels before comparing when the compressor is on/off.

RMS vs. PEAK DETECTION
Peak Mode

Reacts to instantaneous spikes. Great for transient-heavy sources like drums.

RMS Mode

Averages the signal over a short window and reacts more like your ear does. Great for vocals and bass.

A lot of beginners wonder why their compressor feels "slow" even with a fast attack — it's often because it's in RMS mode smoothing out those spikes before reacting.

SIDECHAIN / KEY INPUT

The compressor doesn't have to listen to the signal it's compressing. You can feed it a different signal to trigger the gain reduction.

Ducking

Feed the kick drum into the sidechain of the bass compressor. Every time the kick hits, the bass ducks slightly. Suddenly your low end has space and punch.

De-Essing

A de-esser is literally just a compressor with a sidechain filtered to only hear high frequencies (3–8kHz). When it detects a harsh "S" or "T," it compresses only then.

Vocal Ducking on a Mix

Some engineers sidechain the full mix to duck slightly when the lead vocal peaks, carving out space automatically.

SERIAL COMPRESSION

Instead of one compressor doing heavy lifting — which sounds unnatural — you use two doing lighter work. Pro-level compression happens in stages. Two compressors doing 2dB of work each produces a sound that is rock-solid without ever triggering the listener's brain to think "Oh, that's a compressor."

1ST Slower, gentle ratio (2:1) — catching the big dynamics.
2ND Faster, tighter — shaping the transients.

The result sounds far more transparent and musical than one compressor at 8:1.

PARALLEL COMPRESSION

Blend a heavily compressed signal alongside the dry, uncompressed signal. You get the sustain, thickness, and density of hard compression, but the attack and life of the original transients survive because they're in the dry signal. This is massive on drums and great on vocals that need presence without sounding choked.

COMBINING SERIAL + PARALLEL

These two approaches can be combined, assigning each compressor a single job rather than asking one to do everything.

A Parallel → Serial (drum bus): Thicken up the drums with parallel compression to add density, then send that blended signal into a gentle 2:1 glue compressor. The glue compressor never has to work hard because the wild peaks were already tamed in the blend.
B Serial → Parallel (vocals): A first compressor handles the heavy dynamic control. That tamed signal then gets split into a parallel chain — one side gets a second compressor for color and density, the other stays clean. You're blending controlled + colored against controlled + clean. Common on vocals when you want character without sacrificing clarity.
CHARACTER

The thing that makes an 1176 sound different from an LA-2A lives almost entirely in how the compressor lets go of the signal. The "color," "vibe," and "glue" are the combination of release behavior, harmonic distortion of the circuit, and the detector response all working together.

When people say something sounds "compressed in a good way," they usually mean the release feels musical.

If the release timing matches the natural decay of the instrument, it will be transparent. Whether you want to hear the compressor depends on the music you want to create.
