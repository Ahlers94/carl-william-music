Theory

COMPRESSION

A complete guide from controls to advanced techniques

A COMPRESSOR is a time-based volume automation tool, and the art is making the automation feel like it was performed, not programmed.

When you have a singer that has dynamic range, they might whisper in the verse and belt it in the choruses. So you'd have to manually move the fader down in the chorus. A compressor does that automatically.
The Controls
THRESHOLD

The THRESHOLD knob is like a fader set to the volume that you want the compressor to kick on at. Set it to the level where the loud peaks begin, so the compressor only works when the signal exceeds that point.

ATTACK

ATTACK is the speed in milliseconds at which the compression will kick on once the signal hits the threshold.

Fast Attack (< 5ms)

The compressor catches the very first "crack" of a snare drum or the first consonant of a word. It sounds "tighter" and more controlled.

Slow Attack (> 20-30ms)

The compressor "misses" the initial punch of the sound. This is great for drums or acoustic guitar because it lets the transient (the hit) out before the volume gets pulled down. It makes things sound "punchy."

RELEASE

RELEASE is the speed in ms at which the fader returns to zero gain reduction, fully letting go of the signal.

Fast Release

The gain returns to zero gain reduction quickly. This makes the music feel more "lively" but if it's too fast it creates a "pumping" effect.

Slow Release

The gain returns to zero gain reduction slowly. This makes the music feel "glued" together and smoother, as if the volume is rising and falling with the musical phrasing rather than reacting to every single note.

RATIO

RATIO is the "strength" of the movement. If the ratio is 4:1 think of it this way: 4:1 means that for every 4dB of volume increase past the threshold, it is only increasing by 1dB.

1.5:1 – 2:1 Provides glue, warmth, and is barely noticeable. It's great for bus compression and mixing.
4:1 – 6:1 Typically for vocal and instrument control.
8:1 – 10:1 Getting into limiting territory, very aggressive.
∞:1 Is a limiter. Nothing gets past the threshold, period.
KNEE

KNEE controls the timing of the compressor's transition.

Hard Knee

Instantly applies the full ratio.

Soft Knee

Applies some compression before the signal hits the threshold, gradually increasing the ratio until it hits the full amount. This is good for acoustic guitars or vocals to make the compression feel invisible.

PEAK LIMITER

The PEAK LIMITER is a final safety net. Even if your compressor is doing its job, the LIMITER is at the end of the signal chain to prevent digital clipping or blowing your speakers if anything tries to exceed the value you set.

GATE

The GATE acts like a "silence" switch. If the signal falls below a certain volume, it cuts it off entirely (e.g., stopping the hum of a guitar amp between notes). This is usually put before the compressor in the signal chain.

EXPANDER

The EXPANDER is like a "gentle" gate. Instead of cutting the sound to zero, it just turns it down further when it gets quiet. It's great for cleaning up background noise in a vocal track without the "choppy" sound a hard gate can sometimes create.

GAIN REDUCTION METER

Look at the GAIN REDUCTION METER while you listen.

▼ If you barely see it moving then the threshold is too high or you're barely sending any signal.
▲ If you're pinned in the red or pinned to the bottom your threshold is too low or your ratio is too aggressive, so you're limiting your signal.
◆ The best place to be is when the meter moves up and down with the music and pumps. It should move in time with you.

The timing of that meter movement is as important as the depth. Watch it:

▸ Does it pump in time with the quarter note? The bar? That's your release interacting with the tempo.
▸ On a vocal, does it duck hard on consonants and release on vowels? You may need to slow your attack.
▸ Is it barely moving on the verses but pinned in the chorus? That's normal on a dynamic vocal and you may want to automate the threshold between sections rather than compromise both.
Advanced Techniques
MAKEUP OR OUTPUT GAIN

When compression pulls down peaks, your overall perceived loudness drops. The OUTPUT or MAKEUP GAIN knob is how you push it back up to unity level or beyond.

A huge rookie mistake is bypassing the compressor and thinking it sounds better. It almost always does because the uncompressed signal is louder and louder sounds better to our ears. You should always match levels before comparing when the compressor is on/off.

RMS vs. PEAK DETECTION
Peak Mode

PEAK mode reacts to instantaneous spikes which is great for transient-heavy sources like drums.

RMS Mode

RMS mode averages the signal over a short window and reacts more like your ear does which is great for vocals and bass.

A lot of beginners wonder why their compressor feels "slow" even with a fast attack. It's often because it's in RMS mode smoothing out those spikes before reacting.

SIDECHAIN / KEY INPUT

The compressor doesn't have to listen to the signal it's compressing. You can feed it a different signal to trigger the gain reduction.

Ducking

Feed the kick drum into the sidechain of the bass compressor. Every time the kick hits, the bass ducks slightly. Suddenly your low end has space and punch.

De-Essing

A de-esser is literally just a compressor with a sidechain filtered to only hear high frequencies (3-8kHz). When it detects a harsh "S" or "T," it compresses only then.

Vocal Rides on a Mix

Some engineers sidechain the full mix to duck slightly when the lead vocal peaks.

SERIAL COMPRESSION

Using two compressors in a row. Instead of 1 compressor doing heavy lifting, which sounds unnatural, you use 2 doing lighter work. Pro-level compression happens in stages.

By using Serial Compression (two compressors doing 2dB of work each), you get a sound that is rock-solid and steady without ever triggering the listener's brain to think, "Oh, that's a compressor."

1ST Slower, gentle ratio (2:1), catching the big dynamics.
2ND Faster, tighter, shaping the transients.

The result sounds far more transparent and musical than one compressor at 8:1.

PARALLEL COMPRESSION

Blend a heavily compressed signal alongside the dry, uncompressed signal. You get the sustain, thickness, and density of hard compression, but the attack and life of the original transients survive because they're in the dry signal. This is massive on drums and great for vocals that need presence without sounding choked.

COMBINE SERIAL AND PARALLEL COMPRESSION

You can of course combine these techniques.

A Parallel → Serial (drum bus): Take a parallel blend and send it into a second compressor. Thicken up the drums with the parallel compression and then add a gentle 2:1 glue compressor after.
B Serial → Parallel (vocals): Try a serial compressor into a parallel one. The first compressor handles the heavy dynamic control, then that tamed signal gets split into a parallel chain where one side gets a 2nd compressor for color and density, the other stays clean. You're blending controlled and colored against controlled and clean. This is a common approach on vocals when you want character without sacrificing clarity.
CHARACTER

CHARACTER is the thing that makes an 1176 sound different from an LA-2A and it lives almost entirely in how the compressor lets go of the signal. The "color", "vibe", and "glue" are the combination of release behavior, the harmonic distortion of the circuit, and the detector response all working together.

When people say something sounds "compressed in a good way," they usually mean the release feels musical.

If the release timing matches the natural decay of the instrument, it will be transparent. Whether you want to hear the compressor depends on the music you want to create.
