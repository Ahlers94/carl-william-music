Commercial MIDI controllers are expensive. I wanted a 9-knob performance rig that was reliable, latency-free, and matched my mad scientist vibe without relying on an off the shelf solution. I searched through my parts and found the perfect solution.

The Radionics MIDI Box is an exercise in engineering minimalism. I knew I bought it for a reason. I gutted out the pseudoscientific wire-wrapped crystal nonsense and purchased an ATmega32U4 (Pro Micro) microcontroller. The chip acts as a native USB MIDI device, meaning the Raspberry Pi recognizes it immediately as a plug-and-play instrument.

The 3x3 grid of 50K potentiometers gives me the controls I need to make crucial adjustments. Fully analog to digital (1024 values)

NO MIDDLE-MEN. NO LATENCY. NO COMPROMISE.
JUST NATIVE MIDI AT YOUR FINGERTIPS.

The build process focused on eliminating failure points. The Pro Micro was selected for its small footprint and native Analog to Digital Conversion capabilities, allowing me to wire all 9 variable resistors directly to the chip without needing extra multiplexer hardware.

A custom firmware loop with integrated "deadzone" logic cleans up the signal, ensuring that my performance settings stay locked in place until I decide to twist them. It’s clean, it’s stable, and it’s my own creation.

  • Brain: ATmega32U4 (Pro Micro)
  • Inputs: 9x 50K Potentiometers
  • Connectivity: Native USB Class-Compliant
  • Chassis: $1 Radionics Case
  • Protocol: MIDI CC (1:1 Mapping)
  • Firmware: Custom C++ / MIDIUSB
  • Integration: MODEP / ALSA
  • Philosophy: Reuse and combine parts to create a solution

On stage, it’s a piece of science gear from another era. In the mix, it’s a high-precision performance tool.
