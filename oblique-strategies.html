What Are Oblique Strategies?

Oblique Strategies is a set of cards created by musician and producer Brian Eno and artist Peter Schmidt, first published in 1975. Each card carries a short, often cryptic instruction such as an aphorism, a question, or a constraint designed to dislodge the creative mind from habitual thinking when a project has stalled.

The deck grew from two parallel but independent practices. Schmidt had been collecting similar maxims on cards as part of his own painting discipline throughout the early 1970s. Eno, working in recording studios and finding that deadline pressure caused musicians to retreat to safe, predictable choices, was developing his own parallel set of prompts. When the two discovered the overlap in their methods, they formalized it as a printed deck.

The first edition of 113 cards was a limited print run. Subsequent editions followed in 1978, 1979, and 2001, each revised slightly, with some cards retired and new ones added. The title itself is the method: approach the problem obliquely, from an unexpected angle, rather than head-on. A card might instruct you to "Honor thy error as a hidden intention" or simply "Work at a different speed." The apparent absurdity is deliberate; it forces the brain out of problem-solving mode and into lateral association.

Eno deployed the deck extensively in his production work throughout the late 70s and 80s. Sessions for David Bowie's Heroes and Lodger, Talking Heads' Fear of Music and Remain in Light, and Devo's Q: Are We Not Men? all reportedly involved the cards at moments of creative friction. Eno would draw a card and apply it literally to whatever the band was working on, sometimes to the musicians' frustration, always to productive effect.

The underlying philosophy draws on cybernetics and systems theory: the idea that a system under stress benefits from a random perturbation to escape a local minimum. Creativity, like any complex system, can get trapped in loops. The cards act as a stochastic signal, noise introduced intentionally to break the loop. Whether or not you follow the instruction literally matters less than the interruption it creates in your current mental model.

Today the method is taught in music production programs, design schools, and creative writing courses worldwide. The cards remain in print, and the approach has inspired countless analogous toolkits across disciplines. Their core insight holds: the constraint you didn't choose is often the most liberating one.
